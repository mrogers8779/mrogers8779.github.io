House-Level Mortality Analysis

Surfacing Reporting Gaps Farm-Level Aggregates Hide

A note on the data: This project uses a synthetic dataset built to mirror the structure of real production data I worked with as Assistant Production Controller/Analyst at Prestage Farms — batches, houses, farms, hatcheries, and daily mortality counts. No proprietary or confidential employer data is used. The schema, analytical approach, and findings reflect a real gap I identified on the job but wasn't able to fully build out at the time.

Background

The Problem

Standard production reporting at my previous role provided pig mortality and population data only at the farm level. That made it difficult to catch localized issues, refine driver load scheduling (which depends on accurate live population counts), or evaluate individual nursery house performance. I identified this gap on the job but wasn't able to fully build it out at the time — this project revisits and completes that analysis independently.

Method

Approach

Using SQL Server, I built a layered analysis:

  • Aggregated placement and mortality data at the nursery house level rather than farm level, using CTEs to avoid join fan-out errors between placement and mortality records
  • Calculated mortality rate as a percentage of head placed per house, with divide-by-zero protection via NULLIF
  • Built a window-function-based cumulative mortality view (SUM() OVER PARTITION BY), normalized to "day of batch" so houses with different placement dates could be compared on a common timeline

Visualized in Power BI across two report views: a farm/house mortality overview, and a mortality-timing comparison across flagged houses.

Sample query — house-level mortality summary with fan-out-safe aggregation:

WITH TotalMort AS ( SELECT GroupKeyID, SUM(HeadMort) AS TotalMort FROM Mortality GROUP BY GroupKeyID ) SELECT g.FarmName, g.HouseID, SUM(g.HeadPlaced) AS TotalHead, SUM(t.TotalMort) AS OverallMort, CAST(SUM(t.TotalMort) * 100.0 / NULLIF(SUM(g.HeadPlaced),0) AS DECIMAL(10,2)) AS MortalityPct FROM Groups AS g JOIN TotalMort AS t ON g.GroupKeyID = t.GroupKeyID GROUP BY g.FarmName, g.HouseID

Results

Findings

Farm-level reporting masked meaningful variation. 4 of ~28 houses ran mortality rates clearly above the norm, despite their parent farms appearing unremarkable in aggregate.

A consistent U-shaped mortality pattern emerged across flagged houses: an early spike shortly after placement, a stable middle period, and a second rise late in the batch.

This points to two distinct operational factors, not one — early mortality is more consistent with placement stress on newly weaned pigs, while late-stage mortality is more consistent with house density or ventilation limitations as pigs approach market weight. Both also affect average pig size, and therefore how many head safely fit on a transport load.

Next Steps

Recommendation

House-level mortality tracking should be a standard, recurring report rather than an ad hoc query — it surfaces problems farm-level aggregates hide, and day-of-batch normalization makes houses directly comparable regardless of placement date. Given the distinct early/late mortality pattern, flagged houses warrant separate review of placement/weaning conditions and late-stage housing density, rather than a single blanket fix. More accurate house-level population data would also directly improve load-scheduling precision — the original motivation for this project.
